# Triagegeist: Auditing When Synthetic Emergency Department Triage Benchmarks Hide Residual Risk Signal

Triagegeist is a three-dataset benchmark audit of emergency department triage labels as predictors of patient outcomes. It asks whether models using triage-time features, but excluding the assigned acuity label, can outperform acuity-only baselines for admission/transfer and long ED stay. The same protocol is applied to the hackathon synthetic dataset, NHAMCS 2022, and MIMIC-IV-ED, allowing the synthetic benchmark finding to be tested against two real-data sources.

On the synthetic dataset, the outcome model added only limited value over acuity alone. For admission/transfer, average precision increased from 0.661 to 0.688; for long stay, it increased from 0.710 to 0.733. However, the model lost on balanced accuracy and ROC-AUC, and within acuity bands 3 and 4 there was no clear residual signal, with out-of-fold ROC close to 0.50. Read alone, this would suggest that the acuity label absorbs most outcome-relevant triage information.

The real-data replications challenged that conclusion. In NHAMCS 2022, the model excluding the triage-immediacy label (IMMEDR) reached AP 0.500 for admission/transfer compared with 0.303 for IMMEDR alone, and AP 0.608 for long stay compared with 0.503 for IMMEDR alone. In MIMIC-IV-ED, processed locally under credentialed PhysioNet access, a strict triage-time LightGBM model outperformed ESI acuity-only baselines by an AP gain of +0.173 for admission/transfer and +0.086 for long stay across approximately 418,000 ED stays. Within ESI 3 and ESI 4, residual signal remained clear, with admission out-of-fold ROC reaching 0.784 and 0.814 respectively. The under-triage exhibit further shows that approximately 37% of ESI-3 stays ended in admission or transfer.

The contribution is methodological. Triagegeist does not claim to deploy a clinical triage model. Instead, it demonstrates that a synthetic benchmark can produce a negative finding that does not replicate on real emergency department data. This matters because operational planners are often concerned with exactly this residual signal: which patients within the same apparent acuity category are still likely to require a bed, transfer, or prolonged ED capacity.

Governance and leakage controls are explicit. No raw MIMIC rows, subject IDs, stay IDs, hospital admission IDs, chief complaint text, medication names, or row-level predictions are included in the deliverables. The strict MIMIC model uses triage-time intake features only; diagnoses, disposition, length of stay, Pyxis data, and post-triage vitals are excluded. Reproducibility is supported by seed-locked pipelines, leakage-aware splits, a cleaned submission notebook, and aggregate figures and tables.

Triagegeist is therefore a benchmark audit and proof-of-concept for safer evaluation of emergency triage AI systems. It should not be used for patient care without prospective validation, local governance review, safety testing, and clinician oversight.
